01

The business

Legal & General is a retirement and savings machine in three parts. Institutional Retirement takes over company pension schemes for a premium and pays the pensions itself, the pension risk transfer (PRT) or bulk-annuity business, where L&G is a UK leader. Asset Management is LGIM, a £1.2 trillion manager now pushing into higher-margin private markets. Retail covers workplace pensions, individual annuities and protection. The three feed each other: roughly 80 percent of UK PRT deals come from existing LGIM clients, and workplace savers are funnelled into L&G annuities at retirement. It is a flywheel built on the structural, decades-long shift of pension risk from companies to insurers.

For an investor the appeal is simple and old-fashioned: this is an income stock. The model generates large, fairly predictable capital, and management hands most of it back through a high dividend and now a record buyback. The questions are whether that income is genuinely covered, and whether you are paying a sensible price for it.

02

The income, and whether it is covered

This is the heart of the case, because the answer depends entirely on which number you use. On reported IFRS earnings the dividend looks alarming, a payout ratio of nearly 500 percent, which is where the "value trap, paying from reserves" worry comes from. But IFRS 17 accounting is close to meaningless for a life insurer's economics; statutory profit before tax was just £807m against £1,623m of core operating profit. The number that matters is Solvency II capital generation, the operational surplus the business actually throws off, which was £1.53bn in 2025 and comfortably covers the roughly £1.26bn ordinary dividend about 1.2 times. The £1.2bn buyback on top is funded largely by disposal proceeds and surplus capital, not borrowing.

So on the metric that reflects economics, the income is covered and the balance sheet is strong, a 210 percent Solvency II ratio with a £7.0bn surplus. The honest caveat is the other side of the same coin: capital generation is the thing to watch, and if it ever weakens, the cover the bears already doubt would genuinely come under pressure.

The quality is genuinely good: a return on equity above 20 percent, a £13.3bn store of future profit waiting to be released, and a solvency ratio that, while down from 232 percent as capital is deployed and returned, remains robust. The one earnings-quality point to keep front of mind is the gap between the £1.6bn core operating profit and the £0.8bn statutory figure: judge this company on capital generation, never on its IFRS headline.

04

Management and capital allocation

António Simões, chief executive since early 2024, has spent two years simplifying a famously complex group: selling the US protection business to Meiji Yasuda, forming partnerships with Blackstone and others, and setting a capital-allocation framework with a 14 percent return hurdle for new investment and surplus returned to shareholders. The most debated decision is the dividend pivot: growth deliberately slowed to 2 percent a year, with the freed-up cash redirected into the record £1.2bn buyback. The bull reads this as smart, buying back cheap shares lifts per-share metrics more than a higher dividend would. The bear reads it as a quiet admission that the old mid-single-digit dividend growth was unaffordable. Either way, total returns of more than £5bn over 2025–27 are real, and they are the core of why you own this.

05

Valuation and yield in context

At about 13.6 times core operating earnings L&G is not expensive, though the headline P/E you will see quoted, near 30 times, is the IFRS artefact again and should be ignored. The real draw is the yield. At roughly 7.7 percent it sits far above the FTSE 100, the gilt and the insurance-sector average, and with the buyback on top the all-in cash return is in the mid-teens.

The complication is timing. The shares have rallied from around 243p after the March results to near 285p, close to the 52-week high of 300p, so the yield has compressed from nearly 9 percent to 7.7 percent and analyst targets, clustered in the 260–285p range, now sit around or even below the price. The fat, discounted yield that was on offer three months ago has largely been arbitraged away.

07

The case, both ways

Bull, at its strongest. A ~7.7 percent yield covered by capital generation, a record buyback shrinking the share count, a 210 percent solvency ratio with a £7bn cushion, leadership in a structurally growing PRT market, a £1.2 trillion asset manager finally inflecting, and a return on equity above 20 percent. For an income portfolio, a fairly-secure mid-teens all-in cash return is a rare thing.

Bear, at its strongest. The dividend is uncovered on reported earnings and grows just 2 percent a year, so this is a bond-like equity with little growth. Solvency has fallen from 232 to 210 percent as capital is deployed and returned. Asset management has been a laggard and PRT is getting more competitive. The shares have run to near their highs, the yield has compressed, and the market remains a Neutral, with targets at or below the price. High-yield insurers have been value traps before.

Variant perception

The bear's headline, a dividend covered nearly 500 percent by earnings, is an accounting illusion: IFRS 17 is not how a life insurer's economics work, and on Solvency II capital generation the dividend is covered about 1.2 times with a £7bn surplus behind it. So the real debate is not whether the income is safe, it largely is, but whether the business can grow. Dividend growth has been cut to 2 percent, asset management is unproven, and PRT margins face competition, which makes this a high, fairly-secure yield attached to a low-growth engine, a bond-like equity. That is a perfectly good thing to own for income, but at ~285p near the highs with the yield down to 7.7 percent, the discount that compensated for the complexity at 243p has gone. You are now paying a fair price for the yield, not a cheap one.

08

Currency, for a sterling holder

A welcome change from the rest of the library: L&G is a pure sterling holding. It is quoted in pence, earns the bulk of its profit in pounds, and pays its dividend in pounds. There is no currency translation between the business and your pocket, no dollar exposure to hedge or fret over. For a sterling-based income investor that is a genuine feature, the income arrives in the currency you spend, predictable and unconverted, which is precisely what you want from the ballast in a portfolio.

What would change the call

  • Upgrade to Buy on weakness below ~260p (yield back above ~8.4% with a discount to blended value), or on durable evidence that asset management and PRT are lifting capital generation enough to support faster dividend growth.
  • Downgrade towards Avoid if Solvency II capital generation weakens (threatening cover on the metric that matters), the solvency ratio drifts toward ~180 percent, PRT margins compress materially, or LGIM resumes net outflows.
11

Verdict

A high-quality income holding, fairly valued near its highs after a strong run. ~7.7% yield, covered by capital generation, with a £1.2bn buyback on top. Action: Watch, hold for the income, add below ~260p, not here.

Strip away the IFRS noise and L&G is a sound income compounder: a well-covered 7.7 percent yield, a record buyback, a 210 percent solvency ratio, and a structural tailwind in pension risk transfer. The only problem is the price. After a rally from 243p to near 285p the yield has compressed, the discount has closed, analyst targets sit around the price, and a blended valuation lands right where it trades. That makes this a hold for the income rather than a fresh buy. Collect the dividend and the buyback, and add to the position on weakness, the band below ~260p is where the yield and the margin of safety reopen, with anything above ~325p a place to trim. A constructive watch, and the steadiest, most sterling-pure income in the book.
