01

The business

Glencore is two businesses bolted together. Industrial Activities is a diversified mine portfolio: copper above all, plus zinc, nickel, cobalt, ferroalloys, and a large coal franchise spanning energy and steelmaking coal. Marketing Activities is a global commodity-trading house that sources, ships and sells physical commodities, its own and third parties', and which most pure miners do not have. That trading arm is the differentiator: it earns a relatively steady profit "through the cycle" regardless of where mining margins sit, and management guides it to $2.3bn to $3.5bn of EBIT a year.

The economics are inherently cyclical. Earnings rise and fall with copper, cobalt, zinc and coal prices, which Glencore does not control. The structural bull case is copper, the metal of electrification, where Glencore has a growth pipeline. The structural debate is coal, which it chose to keep rather than spin off, a cash machine that the market discounts on ESG grounds. And the live wildcard is that Rio Tinto opened preliminary merger discussions in January 2026, which is part of why the shares have run.

02

What drives it

For a miner the "operating drivers" are commodity prices and volumes, and 2025 was a tale of two halves and two commodity groups. Metals soared, coal sagged. Copper ran from about $8,500 a tonne in the April tariff scare to roughly $12,500 by year-end; gold, silver and cobalt average prices rose more than 40 percent. Energy coal and steelmaking coal prices fell more than 20 percent. That split, metals up, coal down, is exactly why a diversified book matters.

On volumes, Glencore met production guidance for a second straight year. Copper output of 852kt was 11 percent lower (mine sequencing), but H2 copper jumped almost 50 percent on better grades, and the growth plan targets roughly 1.0Mt near-term, ~1.6Mt with projects, and a stretch above 2Mt by 2035, with unit cash costs falling from ~$1.85 to ~$1.10 per pound as volumes scale. Copper is the engine of the long-term story; coal is the cash that funds it today.

Two things stand out. First, the operating leverage: reported EBITDA was $13.5bn, but at year-end spot prices the run-rate is about $18.1bn, with spot free cash flow near $7bn. A diversified miner is a leveraged bet on commodity prices, and that cuts both ways. Second, the balance sheet constraint: net debt rose to about $14.5bn against a roughly $10bn target, which under Glencore's own returns framework caps additional buybacks and top-up distributions until it comes down. So the income is real but currently throttled, the FY2025 distribution was $0.17 a share, a yield near 2.2 percent, well below the sector, precisely because the balance sheet is above target.

04

Management and capital allocation

Gary Nagle, a Glencore lifer, has been chief executive since 2021, inheriting the trading-house culture of Ivan Glasenberg. Capital allocation runs on a clear framework: fund capex of about $6.5bn a year, pay a base distribution, and top it up with buybacks only when net debt sits below the ~$10bn target. Recent moves show the strategy, lean into copper and trim the rest: acquiring the Quechua copper project, restarting Alumbrera, taking on Teck's steelmaking coal (EVR), while disposing of the Pasar smelter, a Colombian coal terminal, and the Viterra stake, and signing a non-binding deal to sell 40 percent of the DRC copper and cobalt assets to a US-government-backed consortium. The two judgement calls an owner must weigh are the decision to keep coal rather than demerge it, and the governance shadow of the 2022 bribery and market-manipulation settlements of around $1.5bn, which still colours the risk premium.

05

Valuation, in context

On a forward P/E of roughly 14 times recovering earnings and an EV/EBITDA around 6 to 8 times, Glencore is not obviously expensive against mining peers, and on spot EBITDA it is cheaper still, nearer 6 times. The catch is timing. The shares have outperformed the UK market by nearly 50 percent over six months, sit about 32 percent above their 200-day average, and trade near the top of a 273p to 621p range. Analyst targets cluster around 550 to 620p, only low-single-digit percent above the price, so the sell-side, while rated Buy, sees the easy upside as largely captured. You are valuing a cyclical after the cyclical move.

The Rio Tinto overhang

In January 2026 Rio Tinto opened preliminary discussions about a possible all-share merger with Glencore, which would be one of the largest deals in mining history. Part of the share-price rally reflects that speculation. It is a genuine two-way event: a firm bid at a premium is real upside, while collapsed talks could unwind the speculative portion of the price. This is the dominant near-term swing factor, and it is not something a fundamental framework can handicap with any confidence.

07

The case, both ways

Bull, at its strongest. Best-in-class copper leverage into a structural, transition-driven deficit, with a growth pipeline and falling unit costs; a differentiated trading arm that earns through the cycle; a coal book that, however unloved, throws off the cash that funds the copper build and the dividend; strong H2 momentum; and a live Rio Tinto bid that could crystallise value overnight.

Bear, at its strongest. A cyclical bought after it doubled, near its highs, with the upside to analyst targets largely closed. Net debt above target throttling shareholder returns. A coal franchise carrying ESG, regulatory and price risk. A governance record stained by the 2022 settlements. Statutory earnings hit by impairments. And a Rio deal that, if it evaporates, takes the speculative premium with it. Commodity prices, the thing that matters most, are outside anyone's control and currently elevated.

Variant perception

Glencore fuses a copper-growth story the market wants with a coal-and-trading cash machine it discounts on principle. The variant question is whether that coal and trading cash is the liability the ESG screens treat it as, or the underappreciated asset that de-risks the copper build and the balance sheet. Investors who want clean copper avoid Glencore for the coal; but that coal cash is exactly what funds the copper. The second variant is the deal: at 575p near the highs, are you paying for a Rio bid that may not arrive at the hoped-for premium, or underpaying for a transformational combination? Either way, the margin of safety that existed at 273p is gone, and you are now buying the recovery plus a coin-flip on a merger.

08

Currency, for a sterling holder

This one is the opposite of the US names, and worth understanding. Glencore is quoted in pence on the London exchange, so you buy and sell in your own currency with no conversion at the point of trade. But the earnings underneath are in US dollars and driven by dollar-denominated commodity prices, and the dividend is declared in dollars. So a stronger pound against the dollar quietly erodes the sterling value of those earnings and distributions even though the share quote is in pence. The dollar exposure is real but indirect, sitting inside the business rather than on the screen.

What would change the call

  • Upgrade to Buy on a cyclical pullback that restores a margin of safety (toward the high-400s); on net debt falling below the ~$10bn target and unlocking buybacks; or on a firm Rio bid at an attractive premium.
  • Downgrade towards Avoid if metals prices roll over (copper back below ~$9,000), the Rio talks collapse and remove the premium, or net debt rises further and keeps returns suspended.
11

Verdict

Quality assets and real copper optionality, bought late in the move. Through-cycle value ~570p, roughly the price. Action: Watch, not buy at ~575p.

The assets are good and the copper story is genuine, but the discipline with a cyclical is to mind the entry, and this one has already doubled to near its highs, with analyst upside largely closed, returns throttled by above-target net debt, and a binary Rio Tinto situation hanging over it. On a through-cycle blend the shares are worth about what they cost, so there is no margin of safety to compensate for the commodity and deal risk. Wait for a pullback that resets the cyclical entry, or for the merger to resolve. A constructive watch on a good business at an unremarkable price, not a buy here.
