01

The business

Berkshire is not a stock so much as a federation. Beneath the holding company sit dozens of wholly-owned businesses: GEICO and the reinsurance groups in insurance, BNSF in railroads, Berkshire Hathaway Energy in utilities, and a sprawl of manufacturing, service and retail names from Precision Castparts to Clayton Homes. On top of that sits a roughly $290bn portfolio of public stocks, Apple, American Express, Coca-Cola, Bank of America and now a tripled stake in Alphabet, and beneath it all, an insurance float of about $176bn that funds the whole machine at low cost. Crowning it is the largest pile of discretionary cash in corporate America.

The moat is structural and cultural: cost-free insurance leverage, a decentralised model that lets good managers run their own shops, and a balance sheet so strong that Berkshire is the buyer the world calls when it needs $30bn in a hurry. It pays no dividend; it compounds by reinvesting internally, buying businesses and stocks, and occasionally its own shares. The defining fact of 2026 is that, for the first time in sixty years, the person allocating that capital is not Warren Buffett.

02

What drives it

The metric that matters is operating earnings, which Berkshire reports separately precisely because GAAP net income is whipped around by unrealised swings in the equity portfolio. Operating earnings come from four engines: insurance underwriting profit, insurance investment income (booming, because $397bn parked in Treasuries now earns 4 to 5 percent), the regulated cash flows of BNSF and the energy business, and the diversified manufacturing and retail group. In the first quarter of 2026 operating earnings rose 18 percent. The float that funds the investing has grown to $176bn, and it costs Berkshire essentially nothing.

The sixty-year compounding record is one of the great feats in capitalism, though the law of large numbers has begun to bite, the gap over the index has narrowed as Berkshire has grown to a $1tn-plus business. 04

Management, succession and capital allocation

This is the section that matters most, because Berkshire is, at its core, a capital-allocation machine, and the allocator has changed. Greg Abel, who ran the energy business, became chief executive at the start of 2026; Warren Buffett, now 95, stays on as chairman and still controls 38 percent of the votes. Ajit Jain runs insurance; Todd Combs and Ted Weschler manage parts of the equity book. The question every owner must answer is whether this team can allocate capital as well as Buffett did, because that genius, not the businesses, was the engine of the record.

The early evidence is that Abel intends to deploy, not hoard. Within his first months he committed about $10bn to Alphabet, as part of that company's AI-infrastructure raise, and roughly $17bn in two days to homebuilder Taylor Morrison and another housing name, a clear bet on a US housing recovery and a sharp change of pace from Buffett's recent years of net selling and cash building. He has also bought $15m of stock personally and pledged his 2026 salary to buy more, the kind of signal worth noting. Buybacks, Buffett's preferred tool when the shares are cheap, appear paused at today's 1.47× book, which itself tells you management does not regard the stock as a bargain here.

05

Valuation

Berkshire is valued on price-to-book and on the sum of its parts, not on a simple P/E. At roughly 1.47× book it sits in the middle of its historical range, neither the ~1.2× where Buffett used to buy back stock aggressively nor the richer levels of past peaks. The sum-of-parts tells the same story: about $687bn of investments (cash plus the equity portfolio), plus the operating businesses capitalised at a sensible multiple, lands near $1.14tn of value against a market value around $1.05tn. That is a single-digit discount to intrinsic value, real but not wide. The stock has also lagged the market in 2026, so it is cheaper relative to where it was, just not cheap outright.

What the numbers understate is the downside protection: the $397bn of cash and the diversification mean Berkshire falls less than almost anything in a market rout, and that resilience is itself a kind of margin of safety the table cannot show.

07

The case, both ways

Bull, at its strongest. The highest-quality balance sheet in mega-cap equities, near-cost-free float, a decentralised set of cash machines, a sixty-year compounding record, and $397bn of firepower in the hands of a new chief executive who is visibly putting it to work. It trades modestly below the sum of its parts, has lagged the market, and offers genuine downside protection. For a long-term owner, it is the definition of a core holding.

Bear, at its strongest. At $1tn-plus, the size that built the legend now caps the future, the next twenty years cannot resemble the last sixty. Part of the historic valuation was a Buffett premium that must now fade. The cash pile, however comforting, earns Treasury yields while it waits and drags on returns if deployment is slow or poor. And at 1.47× book with only mid-single-digit upside to intrinsic value, you are paying a fair price for a business whose irreplaceable allocator has just left the chair.

Variant perception

The consensus worry is the post-Buffett era and a $397bn cash drag earning T-bill yields while the market compounded. The variant view is that the cash is not dead weight but a written option on the next dislocation: Berkshire is structurally the buyer of last resort, able to write a $30bn-plus cheque when everyone else is a forced seller, as it did with Goldman and GE in 2008. On that reading you are paying a fair price for a high-quality compounder with a built-in put on market stress, and a new chief executive already proving he will deploy. The catch is that this option pays off most when markets fall, so the rational way to own it is to buy more into weakness, not to chase it after the market has recovered, which is exactly where we are.

08

Currency, for a sterling holder

Berkshire trades and reports in US dollars, so your sterling return is the dollar return adjusted for the GBP/USD move over your holding period, which over one to three years can rival the equity return itself. It is an unhedged dollar exposure to size alongside the rest of the portfolio. There is a subtle bonus, though: in a genuine crisis the dollar and Berkshire both tend to behave as safe havens, so for a sterling investor it can be doubly defensive when it matters most. What would change the call

  • Upgrade to Buy on weakness toward ~1.3× book (roughly $430), or in a market selloff that widens the discount and lets Berkshire deploy its cash; or on clear evidence Abel is allocating capital at Buffett-like returns.
  • Downgrade towards Avoid if Abel makes value-destructive acquisitions, the equity portfolio sours badly, or the multiple stays rich above ~1.6× book while the cash sits idle.
11

Verdict

Wonderful business, fortress balance sheet, fair price. Intrinsic value ~$515 against a ~$490 price. Action: Watch, a core-quality name to accumulate on weakness, not to chase here.

Everything about the business is admirable: the float, the cash, the decentralised cash machines, the sixty-year record, and a new chief executive who is already deploying the hoard rather than sitting on it. But the discipline with Berkshire has always been to buy it when others are fearful, because that is when its $397bn of optionality is worth the most, and at ~1.47× book with the market having recovered, that fear is not on offer. You are paying a fair price with only mid-single-digit upside to a conservative intrinsic value. That is a hold-and-accumulate, not an initiate-aggressively. Put it on the list as one of the highest-quality names in the portfolio, size into it on dips toward 1.3× book or in the next selloff, and let the new era prove itself. A constructive watch, with the safest downside in the book.
