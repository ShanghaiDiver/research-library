01

The business

Alphabet is three franchises bolted to a research lab. Google Services, Search, YouTube, Android, Chrome, the Play store and the subscriptions on top, is the cash machine, monetising the world's attention through advertising at extraordinary margins. Google Cloud is the growth engine, now at a $70bn-plus run rate and accelerating as enterprises buy AI compute and tools. And DeepMind plus the Gemini models, the custom TPU silicon, and bets like Waymo make Alphabet the only company that owns the entire AI stack, from chips to models to distribution to a billion-user product surface.

The moat is one of the widest in business: Search is a verb, YouTube has no real rival at scale, and Android plus Chrome give default distribution that competitors pay billions to rent. The standing worry was that generative AI would finally break that moat. So far it has done the opposite, AI Overviews and AI Mode are deepening engagement, and the Gemini app passed 750 million users. The question now is not survival; it is price and capital discipline.

02

What drives it

Alphabet just became the first company to cross $400bn of annual revenue, and growth is accelerating, not fading: the first quarter of 2026 grew 22 percent. Search keeps compounding in the mid-teens despite, or because of, AI; YouTube advertising and the 270-million-plus subscriptions add another double-digit leg; and Cloud is the standout, growing 48 percent in Q4 with operating margins that have leapt past 30 percent and a contracted backlog that has ballooned to roughly $460 billion.

That backlog matters: it is the evidence the AI-cloud demand is contracted, not hoped for. The flip side is what it costs to serve, which is the crux of the whole investment case.

03

Financial quality and the capex bet

The economics are superb, with one large asterisk. Operating margins sit around 33 percent, the balance sheet holds well over $100bn of cash, and free cash flow ran near $74bn. But capital spending is exploding, from $91bn in 2025 to a guided $175–190bn in 2026, nearly double, to build AI data centres. That is crushing near-term free cash flow, accelerating a depreciation wave through the P&L, and, notably, is being part-funded by an $80bn equity raise and a $25bn TPU venture with Blackstone, so existing holders are being diluted to pay for it.

So the single most important judgement is the same one facing every hyperscaler, magnified: will ~$185bn a year of AI infrastructure earn its return? Alphabet has the backlog and the products to argue yes, but 2026 free cash flow will be a fraction of recent years, and the dilution is a real cost the headline numbers hide.

04

Management, capital allocation and the antitrust overhang

Sundar Pichai runs a famously decentralised, engineering-led group; founders Page and Brin retain voting control. Capital allocation has matured, a dividend was introduced and raised, buybacks continue, and the $32bn all-cash Wiz acquisition (cloud security, cleared by the DOJ) is the largest in Alphabet's history. The defining allocation call is the capex bet, funded now with equity and partnerships as well as cash.

The regulatory picture is genuinely mixed and must be held in view. The big one, the DOJ search-monopoly case, resolved far more mildly than feared, no breakup, distribution and traffic-acquisition payments preserved, which is what unlocked the re-rating, though the DOJ and states have appealed. Still live, and more dangerous, is the separate ad-tech case: a court has already found Google illegally monopolised ad-server and exchange markets, and the DOJ is pushing to force a divestiture of the AdX exchange, with a remedies ruling due in 2026. Layer on EU fines, publisher lawsuits, and fresh UK rules aimed at YouTube's under-16 audience, and the legal overhang, while lighter than a year ago, has not gone away.

05

Valuation

This is the heart of the change. Alphabet used to be the cheap megacap, an 18–20× insurance against the AI and antitrust fears. With those fears easing, it has re-rated to about 30 times earnings and over 10 times sales, "priced as a premium growth compounder, not a beaten-down value play." That is dearer than Microsoft (~22×) and Meta (~21×), about level with Amazon, and below Apple (~36×). The recent ~15 percent pullback from the May high of $408 has improved the entry, but it has not restored the old discount. The Street remains strongly positive, 56 buys and no sells, with an average target near $433, but a chunk of that bull case now rests on the capex paying off rather than on a cheap starting multiple.

07

The case, both ways

Bull, at its strongest. The fastest-growing megacap, accelerating to 22 percent, with the only full AI stack, chips, models, cloud and a billion-user distribution surface. Search is growing with AI rather than dying to it, Cloud is compounding at ~48 percent with a $460bn backlog, the search-antitrust threat has been defused, Gemini has gained ground on a stumbling OpenAI, and Waymo is real optionality. At ~30× for ~20 percent growth, the quality is arguably underpriced.

Bear, at its strongest. The valuation cushion is gone, re-rated to ~30× just as ~$185bn of capex, part-funded by dilution, craters near-term free cash flow and stacks up a depreciation wave. The ad-tech case still threatens an AdX divestiture; EU fines, publisher suits and UK youth rules nag at the edges. And the deepest risk is unchanged, only deferred: if AI eventually erodes the ten-blue-links economics, the cash machine funding all of this is the thing at stake.

Variant perception

For years the variant trade was to buy Alphabet's fear-driven discount, betting AI would not kill Search and antitrust would not break it up. That trade has worked, and the discount is gone. The new variant question is whether the market has swung from too-pessimistic to fairly-priced just as the risk profile changes shape: the existential fears recede, but a $185bn capex bet, funded partly by issuing stock, and a live AdX-divestiture case move to the front. The bull says the full-stack moat is uniquely defensible and the spend builds a durable lead. The bear says you have surrendered the valuation cushion exactly when free cash flow is weakest and dilution highest. The honest read: the business has rarely looked stronger, but the easy money, buying the discount, has been made. From here you are paying a fair price for flawless execution on the biggest capital bet in the company's history.

What would change the call

11

Verdict

A wonderful business at a fair price, the closest call in the book. Intrinsic value ~$348 against a ~$344 price. Action: Watch, a high-conviction hold, add on weakness below ~$315.

Alphabet is firing on every cylinder: the fastest-growing of the megacaps, the only full AI stack, Cloud booming with a $460bn backlog, and the two fears that kept it cheap, AI killing Search and a forced breakup, both materially eased. If anything in this library is a near-buy, it is this. What holds it at watch rather than buy is purely the maths: after re-rating to ~30× and a 15 percent pullback, it trades at roughly fair value on a conservative blend, with the upside resting on the bull case the Street strongly backs, and with the valuation cushion gone exactly as ~$185bn of capex and an $80bn dilutive raise crater near-term free cash flow and the ad-tech divestiture case stays live. So: a high-conviction hold and a confident add on any further weakness toward ~$315, where the margin of safety reopens. Of all the watches, this is the one most likely to graduate to a buy on a modest dip.
