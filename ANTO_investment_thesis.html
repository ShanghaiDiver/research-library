01

The business

Antofagasta is a Chilean copper miner in a London listing, controlled (~65%) by the Luksic family. It runs four mines, Los Pelambres, Centinela, Antucoya and Zaldívar, producing around 654,000 tonnes of copper a year, plus valuable by-products of gold, molybdenum and silver that materially lower its net cost, and a transport division serving northern Chile. It is among the lowest-cost, highest-margin pure-play copper producers in the world, and it reports in US dollars because that is the currency of its product.

The appeal is simple and powerful: copper is the metal of electrification, wiring, motors, grids, EVs, renewables and, increasingly, the power infrastructure for AI data centres, while new supply is genuinely hard to bring on (falling ore grades, scarce discoveries, decade-long lead times, water and permitting constraints in Chile). Many forecasters see a structural copper deficit later this decade. Antofagasta is one of the cleanest, best-run vehicles to own that theme. The catch is that it is a price-taker: it does not set the copper price, it lives and dies by it.

02

The leverage, and the volatility it brings

2025 was a record year, and it shows exactly how the leverage works. Revenue rose 30 percent to $8.6bn and EBITDA jumped 52 percent to a record $5.2bn, at a 60 percent margin, while underlying earnings per share leapt from $0.84 to about $1.35. That surge came not from much more copper (production actually fell 2 percent) but from higher prices and strong by-product credits, which drove net cash costs down to a five-year low of $1.19/lb. That is operating leverage: when the copper price rises, the profit rises far faster.

The same leverage runs in reverse, which is why the equity is so volatile. Over the past year the shares have travelled from roughly 1,400p to 4,455p and back to ~3,143p, a ~30 percent fall from the high, with the stock recently flagged as wildly overbought near the top. None of the underlying mines changed much; the copper price and sentiment did. This is the defining feature of owning a miner: the business can be superb and the share price still halve, or double.

03

Growth, costs and the balance sheet

Two things separate Antofagasta from a simple copper proxy: it is low-cost, and it is growing. Production is roughly flat for now, but a heavy investment programme, the Centinela Second Concentrator and Los Pelambres growth projects, is set to lift output materially from around 2027 toward the 900,000-tonne range over the medium term.

The balance sheet is a real strength, net debt just 0.53× EBITDA with $4.9bn of liquidity, which is what lets the group invest $3.4–3.7bn a year and still pay a dividend through the build. But note the honest flag: free cash flow is currently negative because growth capex exceeds operating cash, so the (earnings-covered) dividend is effectively funded from the balance sheet for now. That is a deliberate investment phase, not distress, but it means you are paid little, ~1.5 percent, to wait.

04

Management and the Chile factor

Under CEO Iván Arriagada the operational record is strong: cost discipline, a competitiveness programme delivering $115m of savings, projects broadly on time and budget, and a conservative balance sheet. Family control aligns management with long-term value but means minority holders ride alongside the Luksics rather than steering. The bigger external variable is Chile: the country supplies a quarter of the world's copper, but mining there faces water scarcity (Antofagasta has invested heavily in desalination), long permitting timelines, and periodic debate over royalties and taxation. Zaldívar's life was just extended to 2051, a reminder that securing permits is itself a core competency here.

05

Valuation

Valuing a miner on a P/E is close to meaningless, they look dear at mid-cycle and cheap at the peak, and Antofagasta's headline ~24–28× simply reflects that copper is the favoured metal and growth is coming. The more honest statement is that the equity is a leveraged bet on the long-run copper price, and small changes in that assumption swing the value enormously. The analyst community illustrates this perfectly: a Neutral consensus, an average target around 3,500p (~11 percent above the price), but a range running from ~2,356p to ~4,852p. That spread, the high target double the low, is not sloppiness; it is the irreducible uncertainty of forecasting copper.

07

The case, both ways

Bull, at its strongest. The best structural demand story in the library, electrification plus AI-power copper intensity against a wall of supply constraints, owned through one of the lowest-cost, highest-margin pure-play producers, with a fortress balance sheet and ~40 percent production growth coming from sanctioned projects. If the copper super-cycle is real, this is among the finest ways to own it.

Bear, at its strongest. It is a price-taker that just swung 30 percent on sentiment, trading at a full multiple with no free cash flow and a ~1.5 percent yield, so you are not paid to wait through copper's inevitable drawdowns. Chilean water, permitting and tax risk are real, capex is at a peak, and "structural deficit" has been forecast, and delayed, before. A recession or a China wobble could halve the metal's near-term price regardless of the long-term story.

Variant perception

The copper-deficit thesis is now consensus, and Antofagasta is the agreed quality vehicle for it, which is precisely the risk. The variant point is that being right about copper's decade and right about this entry are two different things. The structural case is probably sound directionally, but secular bull markets in commodities still contain violent 30–50 percent drawdowns (recession, Chinese destocking, a supply surprise), and here you hold a leveraged claim that pays you almost nothing, FCF-negative, ~1.5 percent yield, to sit through them. The business quality, low cost and a strong balance sheet, buys survival and the optionality to keep investing through a downturn; it does not buy immunity from the cycle, as the past year's price chart shouts. So the honest read: if you believe in the copper super-cycle, own Antofagasta, but treat it as a leveraged, volatile bet to be accumulated on the metal's washouts rather than chased at a full multiple near a high. The recent ~30 percent pullback is a move in that direction, not yet a bargain.

08

Currency, for a sterling holder

An unusual case: Antofagasta reports in US dollars (copper's currency, Chilean operations) but is quoted in pence and declares its dividend in US cents. So under a pence quote sits a dollar business, and your sterling return carries two layers of currency: the copper price (in dollars) and GBP/USD. A weaker pound lifts the sterling value of a dollar-earning business, all else equal, while the dividend is a dollar amount you receive converted to pounds. In practice you are holding a dollar copper play wearing a sterling jacket, similar in mechanics to IHG, so do count this as dollar, not domestic, exposure when sizing it against your other holdings.

What would change the call

  • Upgrade to Buy on a copper-driven selloff toward or below ~2,900p (the leverage that hurts on the way down is what creates the entry), or as the growth projects ramp and free cash flow turns clearly positive while the price lags.
  • Downgrade towards Avoid on signs the copper cycle is rolling over (recession, Chinese demand collapse, a supply surge), a Chilean tax or permitting shock, or project cost/schedule overruns, especially with the shares still near a high.
11

Verdict

A superb miner and the cleanest way to own copper, which is not the same as a cheap or safe stock. Intrinsic value ~3,500p (wide) against a ~3,143p price. Action: Watch, a quality copper vehicle, accumulate on the metal's weakness, size for the swings.

Antofagasta is genuinely excellent at what it does: low-cost, high-margin, conservatively financed, growing, and pointed straight at the strongest structural demand story in the book. If the copper super-cycle plays out, few equities express it better. But none of that changes what it fundamentally is, a leveraged claim on a volatile price the company does not control, trading at a full multiple with no free cash flow during its build phase and paying only ~1.5 percent to wait. The past year, a round trip from 1,400p to 4,455p to 3,143p, is the whole risk in one line. So it is a watch, not a buy at this level: own it if you want copper exposure and can stomach the volatility, accumulate on the inevitable copper washouts toward ~2,900p rather than chasing strength, and trim into spikes above ~4,200p. The ~30 percent pullback from the high has improved the odds; it has not turned a leveraged commodity bet into a margin-of-safety purchase.
