01

The business

Rolls-Royce makes the engines and power systems that sit at the safety-critical heart of aircraft, warships, submarines and data centres. Civil Aerospace builds and, crucially, services the big engines on widebody jets, earning for decades after the sale through long-term service agreements, the "power-by-the-hour" model where revenue rises with engine flying hours. Defence supplies military engines, naval propulsion and submarine nuclear reactors, including for the AUKUS programme. Power Systems, the mtu brand, makes the diesel and gas systems increasingly in demand for data-centre backup power. And a fourth leg, small modular nuclear reactors, is moving from concept to order book.

The moat is the installed base and the certification barrier: once a Rolls engine is on a wing, it generates high-margin aftermarket revenue for thirty years, and the regulatory and engineering hurdles to displacing it are immense. Three years ago this was a near-bankrupt company. Today, under Tufan Erginbilgiç's transformation, it is one of the highest-quality industrials in the FTSE. The only open question is what you should pay for it.

02

The turnaround, in numbers

The scale of the operational change is hard to overstate. Underlying operating profit has roughly doubled in two years to £3.5bn at a 17.3 percent margin, free cash flow reached £3.3bn, and the balance sheet has swung from heavy net debt to £1.9bn of net cash. Management has already guided 2026 into the range it originally set for the mid-term, two years early, and then raised the 2028 targets again.

The quality is excellent and the cash is real. 04

Management and capital allocation

Tufan Erginbilgiç, chief executive since January 2023, arrived calling Rolls a "burning platform" and has delivered a textbook turnaround: cost out, commercial contracts repriced, margins lifted across all divisions, and a target-and-beat cadence that has rebuilt credibility. Capital allocation is now a happy problem, fund growth (UltraFan, SMR, capacity), pay a reinstated and growing dividend, and return the surplus through a £7–9bn buyback, all from net cash. The risk to keep in mind is that the easy phase of the turnaround, the self-help margin gains, is largely banked. The next leg of growth has to come from end-market expansion and flawless execution, which is a higher bar than fixing your own cost base.

05

Valuation

This is where the case gets hard. At ~1,400p Rolls trades around 35 times forward earnings and closer to 47 times trailing underlying earnings, on roughly 25 times EBITDA, near an all-time high. The dividend yield is a token 0.7 percent, so the cash return comes from the buyback, worth a little over 2 percent a year. Tellingly, the shares have compounded far faster than the (very fast) earnings: a large part of the fifteen-fold move has been the multiple expanding, not just profits rising. The Street remains a Buy, 16 of 20 analysts, but the average target of about 1,420p is barely 1 percent above the price, with the range running 1,101 to 1,740p. The market has, in other words, already paid up for the structural-growth story; from here you are relying on earnings coming through rather than on any further re-rating.

07

The case, both ways

Bull, at its strongest. A transformed, net-cash business with four structural growth legs, civil aftermarket on a younger fleet, defence into a rearmament cycle, data-centre power, and small modular reactors, all underpinned by a target-and-beat management team and a £7–9bn buyback. Quality compounders with multi-decade tailwinds rarely look cheap, and Rolls keeps raising the bar.

Bear, at its strongest. You are paying ~35 times forward and ~47 times underlying earnings at an all-time high, after a fifteen-fold run in which the multiple did much of the work. Civil aerospace is cyclical and a recession would cut flying hours; supply chains are still constrained; nuclear permitting is slow; the Middle East outlook is uncertain. The Street's average target offers ~1 percent upside. There is simply no margin of safety, and the cycle always turns eventually.

Variant perception

Bull and bear agree the business is transformed; the entire debate is valuation and durability. The variant question is whether Rolls is a cyclical aerospace recovery that has overshot and will mean-revert, or a structural compounder at the start of a multi-decade run, defence rearmament, data-centre power, nuclear and a growing aftermarket, that makes the old cyclical framing obsolete. The bull's strongest point is that these four legs are largely independent, so the whole is more resilient than any aerospace cycle. The bear's is that at ~47 times underlying earnings the price already capitalises a decade of that thesis going right, leaving nothing for the supply-chain, cyclical or geopolitical stumbles that aerospace inevitably throws up. You are paying the structural-compounder multiple for a business that, if it turns out to be merely an excellent cyclical, has a long way to fall. That asymmetry, not the quality, is why this is a watch.

08

Currency, for a sterling holder

Rolls is quoted in pence and reports in pounds, so the screen and the dividend need no conversion. But unlike a domestic insurer, a large slice of its revenue, aero-engines and services, is priced in US dollars while much of its cost and engineering base is in sterling, so a weaker pound actually flatters reported profit and a stronger pound trims it. The group hedges a multi-year book of this exposure, which smooths but does not remove it. For a sterling holder the practical point is mild: no conversion friction on the quote or the dividend, but the earnings carry an embedded, partly-hedged dollar sensitivity sitting inside the business.

What would change the call

11

Verdict

A great business that has become a great stock, and the price now reflects it. Intrinsic value ~1,310p against a ~1,400p price. Action: Watch, admire and add on weakness, do not chase at the high.

Rolls-Royce is one of the highest-quality industrials in the index, with a fortress balance sheet, a target-beating management team and four genuine structural tailwinds. None of that is in dispute. What is in dispute is the price: at ~1,400p near an all-time high it trades at ~35 times forward and ~47 times underlying earnings, having risen far faster than even its rapid profits, with the Street's average target barely above the quote. There is no margin of safety, and aerospace is a cyclical industry whose downturns arrive without invitation. So this is a watch, not a buy here: hold and admire if you own it, accumulate on a genuine pullback below ~1,150p, and trim into strength above ~1,550p. Let the earnings grow into the multiple rather than paying ahead of them, the business is excellent enough to be worth waiting for a better entry.
