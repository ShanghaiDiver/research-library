01

The business

Apple is the most valuable company on the planet, and it got there selling roughly the same four things it sold a decade ago, only at higher prices and with a fast-growing toll booth attached. The hardware, iPhone above all, then Mac, iPad, Watch and AirPods, is the foundation. The Services layer sold into that installed base, the App Store, iCloud, payments, advertising, search licensing and subscriptions, is the profit engine bolted on top.

The moat is the installed base and the switching costs around it: hardware, software and services interlock, the brand carries genuine pricing power, and leaving is a chore most people never undertake. Two dependencies are worth naming. A large, very high-margin slice of Services is the fee Google pays to remain the default search engine on Apple devices, and that sits squarely in the path of antitrust remedies. And the company everyone says will be defined by the next decade of AI has chosen to outsource the frontier of it, leaning on a Google partnership to power the Siri overhaul rather than building the models itself.

02

Operating drivers

Beneath the financials sit a handful of real drivers. Apple is still, fundamentally, an iPhone company: the phone is half of all revenue and grew only 4 percent in FY25. Services is the part that matters for the future, a quarter of revenue compounding in the mid-teens at roughly double the gross margin of hardware, which is why it punches well above its revenue share in profit. Everything else is a supporting act.

Geographically the concentration is in the Americas (43 percent of sales) and Europe (27 percent). Greater China, long the swing factor, is 15.5 percent and shrinking, down again in FY25 on weaker iPhone sales and propped up partly by government subsidies. Japan (7 percent) and the rest of Asia Pacific (8 percent) are growing double digits but are too small to offset a China stumble.

A KPI caveat worth knowing

Apple stopped reporting unit volumes in 2018, so there are no iPhone units or average selling prices to track; you see revenue by category and geography, the products-versus-services margin split, and an installed base Apple now describes only qualitatively. That base hit another all-time high in FY25 across every category and region (last quantified in the region of 2.35bn active devices). The disclosure is thinner than an analyst would like, and that itself is a fact about the stock.

Two honest adjustments to the headline. First, growth is the soft spot: revenue shrank in FY23, crawled in FY24, and the recent re-acceleration to 16 to 17 percent is partly flattered by an unusually low tax rate, so do not annualise it. Second, free cash flow overstates what reaches owners, because share-based compensation, now $12.9bn a year and rising, is added back as non-cash yet dilutes you for real. Owner earnings, free cash flow less SBC, are nearer $86bn to $116bn than the headline $99bn to $129bn, and that lower figure is the right input for valuation.

The balance sheet is a fortress: total debt around $98.7bn against a net cash position, so net debt to EBITDA is effectively nil. Capex of only about $13bn against that cash generation funds a token dividend and an enormous buyback.

The look ahead

Consensus expects the recent acceleration to carry through the current year before normalising. The bull narrative is coherent: an AI-driven iPhone upgrade cycle if Siri finally ships, Services compounding at high margin, and gross margin pushing past 48 percent. The capex-light model means most of that profit converts to cash, unlike peers spending $125bn to $190bn a year on AI infrastructure.

05

Management, succession and capital allocation

The biggest governance event in years is in train. On 20 April 2026 Apple announced that Tim Cook will move to executive chairman on 1 September 2026, with John Ternus, the 50-year-old head of hardware engineering and a 25-year Apple veteran, becoming the eighth CEO. The succession reads as orderly and long-planned (Art Levinson stays on as lead independent director), and Ternus carries deep product credibility, his fingerprints are on the iPhone, iPad, AirPods and the Mac's move to Apple silicon. The irony is hard to miss: a hardware engineer inherits the chair precisely when the company's most-questioned weakness is software and AI. That is either reassuring product focus or the wrong skillset for the moment, and it is a genuine open question, not a settled one.

On capital allocation, the record is enormous and double-edged. Apple has spent roughly $470bn on buybacks over five years, shrinking the share count from 16.7bn to under 15bn, and returned about $112bn in FY25 alone (buybacks plus dividend), more than it generated in free cash flow, which is why the net cash pile has drifted down. Those buybacks were struck mostly well below today's price and have been the main engine of per-share earnings growth. But repurchasing stock at 36 times earnings and a sub-3 percent free cash flow yield is a far lower-return use of cash than it was at 18 to 20 times, and a disciplined allocator would at least question whether buying back at this multiple still creates value. Governance is otherwise shareholder-friendly: Apple holds no employment contracts and guarantees no severance or equity acceleration for executives. Cook's FY25 pay was about $74m.

Valuation, in context

Against its own history Apple is dear: a trailing P/E near 36 sits at the top of its five-year range, with EV/EBITDA near 27 and P/FCF near 34 likewise at their ceilings. Against peers it is the most expensive of the mega-caps while not the fastest grower.

Intrinsic value: a discounted cash flow

The DCF uses a normalised starting free cash flow, two-stage growth fading over ten years to a 3 percent terminal rate, a discount rate centred on 9 percent, and net cash added across 14.69bn shares.

On headline free cash flow even a sustained 12 percent tops out near $247; on the owner-earnings base, which strips out share comp, the same grid runs roughly $150 to $220. Dropping the discount rate to 8 percent lifts these; raising it to 10 percent cuts them. Run backwards, today's $298 demands about 14 to 15 percent free cash flow growth for five years at a 9 percent rate. Against consensus growth fading from 15 to 8 percent, that is the optimistic edge of plausible priced as the base case.

On trusting this

A DCF is assumption-driven. The free cash flow base is genuinely ambiguous (owner earnings ~$116bn, headline FCF ~$130bn, FY26 consensus ~$140bn) and the discount rate is a judgement. The same model flips from clearly overvalued at 9 percent to roughly fair at 8 percent with sustained double-digit growth. On a mega-cap, treat it as a sanity check, not a precision instrument.

08

The case, both ways

Bull, at its strongest. Best-in-class and still-improving margins, an installed-base moat with real pricing power, a genuine recent re-acceleration, a Services mix that keeps lifting profitability, a fortress balance sheet, and a capex-light model that converts to enormous cash and steady per-share shrinkage. If Siri ships and drives the upgrade cycle, the premium is defensible.

Bear, at its strongest. Valuation leaves no margin for error at 36×. The Siri overhaul has slipped repeatedly, landing as a beta at WWDC with no firm ship date. Greater China is ~15.5 percent of revenue and already shrinking, exposed to tariffs, Taiwan and rare-earth leverage. iPhone is still half the company and grew only 4 percent. Regulation threatens both the App Store and the lucrative Google search payment.

Variant perception

The market is paying ~36× for AI optionality. The question that actually matters is whether Apple's outsource-the-models strategy is a fatal lag or a shrewd, capital-light play, and whether a hardware engineer is the right CEO to settle it. Believe Apple monetises AI through the installed base without the $150bn capex bill peers carry, and the premium is cheap. Believe it is structurally behind, and you are overpaying for a melting option. The framework cannot resolve that for you; it makes sure you have decided which side you are on before you pay 36×.

Currency, for a sterling holder

Apple reports and trades in US dollars. Your return in sterling is the dollar return adjusted for the GBP/USD move over your holding period, and over one to three years that move can rival or exceed the equity return itself. A stronger pound erodes your dollar gains; a weaker one flatters them. This holding carries an unhedged dollar exposure that should be sized alongside the rest of the portfolio's currency mix and watched as a live variable. The live GBP/USD can be added on request.

12

Verdict

Quality: exceptional. Price: demanding. Intrinsic value: ~$150–250, weighted ~$210. Action: Watch, do not initiate at $298.

Three independent angles point the same way on price. Apple trades at the top of its own valuation range; it is the dearest of the mega-caps while growing slower than several; and a cash-flow valuation, especially once share comp is treated as the real cost it is, says you are paying $298 for something worth closer to $210 unless the bull case sustains for five years. The discipline is to set a buy level in the high-20s P/E (~$230–250), where the overpayment disappears and the AI rollout becomes upside you are not paying full freight for, and to scale in on weakness rather than chase. Not a "do not buy ever", and certainly not a "buy here". A patient, well-defined watch.
