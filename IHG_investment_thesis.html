Buy/sell guide: accumulate below ~$130 (low-20s forward multiple, a margin of safety); trim above ~$160 (~28×+ forward), where the price runs ahead of the model. At ~$168 the stock sits in the trim zone, a reasonable point to bank some gain.

01

The business

IHG does not, for the most part, own hotels. It owns brands, twenty of them, from InterContinental, Regent and Six Senses at the top to Holiday Inn, Holiday Inn Express and Crowne Plaza in the middle, and it collects fees from the owners who franchise or pay it to manage those properties. That is the asset-light model: roughly 7,000 hotels and over a million rooms across more than 100 countries, run not as a property company but as a royalty stream on other people's real estate, bound together by the IHG One Rewards loyalty programme of more than 160 million members.

The moat is brand, distribution and loyalty. Owners sign with IHG because its brands and booking channels fill rooms, 83 percent of room revenue comes through IHG's own channels and two-thirds of room nights from loyalty members, which lowers their cost of customers. Because IHG carries little of the property risk, the economics are extraordinary: fee margins above 64 percent, cash conversion above 100 percent, and almost all the cash handed back to shareholders. The question is never quality. It is price, and where the travel cycle is heading.

02

What drives it

Three levers drive fee revenue: how full the hotels are and at what rate (RevPAR), how many hotels are in the system (net system growth), and the margin on the fees. In 2025 the second and third did the heavy lifting while the first stalled. Net system size grew 4.7 percent on a record 443 openings, the pipeline stands at 2,292 hotels (about a third of current rooms), and fee margin jumped 3.6 points to 64.8 percent. But RevPAR grew only 1.5 percent, and the picture by region is the story: Europe, the Middle East and Asia strong, the Americas flat and decelerating through the year, and Greater China negative.

The bull reads this as resilience: the model delivered double-digit earnings growth on almost no RevPAR, proof it can compound through soft demand. The bear read it as a warning that the US heartland was rolling over. The Q1 2026 update has, for now, settled the argument in the bull's favour: RevPAR re-accelerated to +4.4%, with ADR up 2.0% and occupancy up 1.5 points, and the Americas swung back to growth. The organic engine that looked stalled is turning again, which is much of why the shares have run. The catch, addressed below, is that the price has more than caught up with the better news.

The asset-light economics are the real thing: a 65 percent fee margin and cash conversion above 100 percent mean almost every dollar of profit becomes cash that can be returned. But of the 16 percent adjusted EPS growth, roughly four points came from buybacks shrinking the share count, not from the business growing. That is not a criticism, buybacks are a legitimate and tax-efficient way to compound, but it matters for what you are paying for: at this multiple, a chunk of the "growth" is financial engineering layered on a stalling top line.

04

The look ahead

Management's framework is clear and, on its record, credible: RevPAR plus net system growth drives fee revenue; operating leverage adds 100 to 150 basis points of fee margin a year; cash conversion runs near 100 percent; and the surplus funds a growing dividend plus buybacks, targeting compound adjusted EPS growth of 12 to 15 percent. For 2026 the plan is a $950m buyback and $1.2bn-plus of total returns, part of more than $5bn over five years. The pipeline supports the unit-growth half of the equation for years. The swing factor is RevPAR: if US demand recovers, the algorithm comfortably clears the top of its range; if the US tips into a genuine travel downturn, even this resilient model feels it.

05

Management and capital allocation

Elie Maalouf runs a tight, shareholder-friendly ship. Capital allocation is a model of asset-light discipline: minimal capital into the business (key money of $177m to win owner deals), with the cash returned through a dividend raised 10 percent for a fourth straight year and serial buybacks that have cut the share count meaningfully. The growth spend goes on brands and distribution, recent additions include the urban-lifestyle brand Ruby and the new premium Noted Collection, and on technology and the loyalty platform that feed the ancillary fee streams (co-brand credit cards, loyalty-point sales) now padding the margin. There is little to fault in how the cash is handled; the judgement call for an owner is purely the price paid for it.

06

Valuation, in context

At roughly 29 times earnings IHG is not cheap, and the peer table shows why the comparison cuts both ways. It is dearer than the broad lodging average and the midscale-focused franchisers, but in line with Marriott and below Hilton and Hyatt, the elite global asset-light names it most resembles. So you can call it the cheapest of the premium franchisers or one of the priciest hotel stocks, depending on the comp set you choose.

The dividend yield is modest at about 1.3 percent, because IHG returns far more through buybacks than dividends. Sell-side opinion is genuinely split, a Neutral consensus that unusually includes several outright sell ratings, and the share price has run up to, or slightly past, the average target. The easy upside looks captured.

08

The case, both ways

Bull, at its strongest. One of the best business models in the FTSE: capital-light, 65 percent fee margins, 100 percent-plus cash conversion, a third of its room count already in the pipeline, a 160-million-member loyalty engine, and a management team compounding earnings at 12 to 15 percent while handing back more than $5bn over five years. It proved in 2025 it can grow double digits even with flat RevPAR. Quality this durable rarely comes cheap.

Bear, at its strongest. You are paying ~30 times forward earnings at a fresh all-time high, above every analyst target, for a recovery that may prove a couple of good quarters rather than a trend. RevPAR has bounced, but travel is cyclical, the US can soften again, geopolitics periodically hits the sector, and a UK competition probe into hotel data-sharing still hangs over the group. A chunk of the EPS growth remains buyback-driven. With the Street on a near-unanimous Hold and the price above its targets, the margin of safety is not just thin, it is negative.

Variant perception

For most of the past year the variant question was whether IHG's growth was real demand or financial engineering, with RevPAR stuck near flat. The Q1 2026 bounce to +4.4% has answered it in the bull's favour: the organic engine is turning again. So the variant question has flipped. It is no longer "is the growth real" but "has the market extrapolated two good quarters into a permanent re-rating?" At ~30 times forward and a fresh high, the price now embeds a clean continuation of the recovery; any wobble in US travel or the wider cycle leaves little cushion. The asset-light economics still mean IHG compounds even in a downturn because it does not own the hotels, which is why it deserves a premium, but a premium is not a blank cheque. The discipline is unchanged from the first version of this note, want this wonderful business at a better price, except now the price is higher, not lower, so the live action is to trim rather than wait.

09

Currency, for a sterling holder

This is an unusual one, and the wrinkle is worth stating plainly. IHG's ordinary shares trade on the London exchange in US dollars, not pence, and the company reports and pays its dividend in dollars too. So unlike a normal LSE holding there is no pence quote to soften the currency: as a sterling investor you convert pounds to dollars when you buy, back to pounds when you sell, and the dividend arrives in dollars. The exposure is direct and entirely on-screen, the opposite of Glencore, where the dollar sits hidden inside a pence quote. The practical cost is reinvestment friction, every time you top up the position or recycle a dividend you pay the currency spread again, a small but real drag on compounding a dollar-denominated holding from a sterling base. Worth factoring into position sizing and into how you handle the income, rather than the share-price risk itself.

What would change the call

12

Verdict

A wonderful asset-light business whose price has overshot a genuinely improving story. Intrinsic value ~$148 against a ~$168 price. Action: Watch, and for a holder, trim into strength.

The business is performing: RevPAR has re-accelerated to +4.4%, the pipeline is a third of the room count, fee margins are above 65 percent and the buyback grinds on. That recovery is exactly why the shares have run, and it lifts the fair value from the version I first wrote. But the price has run further: at ~$168 the stock trades at roughly 30 times forward earnings, at a fresh high, above every analyst target and about 12 percent above a blended value. The quality has not changed; the margin of safety has gone, and then some. So the call shifts from "own it cheaper" to "trim into strength", a sensible point for a holder to bank some gain rather than add, with the add zone now down below ~$130. Keep it on the list as one of the best businesses you can own, and let the price come back toward the value, which on this quality it eventually tends to do.

Not personal financial advice; the verdict illustrates the framework's output, not a recommendation to act.
