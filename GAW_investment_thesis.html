Buy/sell guide: accumulate below ~£175 (~17,500p, where a pullback brings the multiple back toward the high-20s and a cushion reopens); trim above ~£230 (~23,000p, beyond the highs and the bull case). At ~£204 it sits in the upper half of the hold zone, near its all-time high.

01

The business

Games Workshop designs, makes and sells miniature wargames, principally Warhammer 40,000 and Age of Sigmar, and it owns the entire universe end to end. It writes the lore, sculpts the models, moulds the plastic in Nottingham, paints, packages and sells through 570 of its own stores, 8,100 independent stockists and online, then sells the customer the paints, brushes, rulebooks and novels to go with it. Roughly 79 percent of core sales are now outside the UK. This is one of the purest cases of vertical integration and owned intellectual property on any exchange.

The result is economics most companies can only dream of: gross margins around 70 percent, an EBITDA margin near 50 percent, returns on capital that are frankly extraordinary, net cash and no debt, and a customer base so devoted the trade affectionately calls the product "plastic crack." On top of the core sits a small but high-margin licensing business, video games like the seven-million-selling Space Marine 2, and now films and television. The question is never the quality. It is always the price.

02

What drives it, and the near-term wrinkle

Two engines. The core business compounds through new stores, more stockists, price rises the devoted base absorbs, and global expansion, core revenue is set to grow about 11 percent in FY2026, to over £625m. The second engine, licensing, is smaller, lumpier and very high margin: it spiked to a record £52.5m in FY2025 on Space Marine 2 and the Amazon signing, a level management openly says will not repeat, normalising back toward £30m in FY2026.

It is an optical pause, not a stall, but it does mean the near-term earnings growth that has to justify a 33× multiple is, for this one year, largely absent.

03

The Amazon option

The wildcard worth its own section. In late 2024 Games Workshop granted Amazon exclusive rights to films and television set in the Warhammer 40,000 universe, with Henry Cavill, a genuine, lifelong fan, attached as star and executive producer, and an option to extend to the Fantasy universe after an initial 40K production. Creative guidelines are agreed and the project is progressing, slowly and under tight wraps.

This matters out of proportion to today's licensing cheque. If Warhammer crosses over the way Tolkien or Marvel did, the prize is not just licence fees, it is a structural step-change in awareness that pulls millions of new hobbyists toward the core product, where the real money is made. That is a genuinely asymmetric upside. It is also unvaluable with any precision and may take years, or may underwhelm. The honest treatment is to regard it as a free-ish call option attached to the shares, not as a number in the model.

The capital-return policy is admirably simple: keep an £85m cash buffer, distribute the rest as dividends, declared in chunky, variable instalments through the year. There are no buybacks and no empire-building, just cash out of the door, with the payout having grown around 31 percent a year over the past decade. The dividend is variable by design rather than a fixed promise, which suits the cash generation but means it flexes with profit.

05

Valuation

Here is the rub. At ~£204, near its all-time high of ~£209 and up about 25 percent on the year, Games Workshop trades around 33 times earnings. For a business of this quality that is not absurd, but it is full, especially in a year when profit is flat. The slim analyst coverage, three houses, is constructive but tellingly so: the average target of about £203 sits essentially on the share price, with even the high estimate (£220) only single digits above. The market has correctly identified a wonderful company and has, for now, priced it as one. There is no discount on offer for the quality, and none for the Amazon option either.

07

The case, both ways

Bull, at its strongest. A peerless, cash-generative monopoly on its own universe, ~50 percent EBITDA margins, vast returns on capital, net cash, a fanatically loyal global base still expanding store by store, and a Hollywood-scale option on the Warhammer IP through Amazon and Cavill. Businesses this good, with this kind of optionality, almost never trade cheaply, and quality compounds.

Bear, at its strongest. You are paying 33× near an all-time high for a year of flat profit, with tariffs biting a UK manufacturer that sells mostly abroad, licensing inherently lumpy, and the Amazon payoff years away and unguaranteed. Analyst targets are already at the price. It remains a niche hobby dependent on one franchise, with a famously hard-nosed stance toward its own community, and the shares carry plenty of momentum-money that can leave quickly.

Variant perception

The bull and bear agree the business is exceptional; they disagree on what the Amazon option is worth and whether you should pay for it now. The variant point cuts a third way: the thing most likely to re-rate the stock, a hit 40K series dragging millions toward the core, is precisely the thing the market cannot price, so it tends to under-weight it until there is footage, then over-weight it on release. That argues for owning the option before it is provable, not after, which is the bull case for buying today. But the same logic argues for discipline on entry: because the option is unvaluable and slow, you want to acquire it with a margin of safety in the core valuation, not at 33× with profit flat and the price already at the analysts' targets. So the honest read is that this is a wonderful business and a fascinating option, fairly priced, where the patient move is to wait for the inevitable wobble, tariffs, a soft release window, a momentum unwind, to own the quality and the option together at a discount, rather than to chase the quality at the high.

08

Currency, for a sterling holder

A sterling quote wrapped around globally-earned profit. Games Workshop is priced in pence and pays its dividend in pounds, so there is no conversion friction for you, but with roughly 79 percent of core sales outside the UK, its reported results swing with sterling: a weaker pound flatters them, a stronger pound, and the new US tariffs, work against. So the holding is clean at the level of your quote and income, but the earnings underneath carry real, unhedged currency and trade-policy sensitivity, much like Rolls-Royce. No conversion to manage; do keep the dollar earnings exposure in mind.

What would change the call

  • Upgrade to Buy on a pullback below ~£175 (~17,500p, a high-20s multiple and a cushion restored), or on tangible evidence the Amazon production is landing while the core keeps compounding double digits.
  • Downgrade towards Avoid if core growth slows materially, tariffs or FX compress the famous margins, the hobby shows saturation, or the multiple stretches further above the mid-30s on flat earnings.
11

Verdict

The best business in the book at a price that knows it. Intrinsic value ~£204 against a ~£204 price. Action: Watch, hold and admire, buy the quality and the Amazon option on a dip below ~£175.

Games Workshop is as close to a perfect business as this library contains: a wholly-owned universe, ~50 percent EBITDA margins, towering returns on capital, net cash, a devoted global base, simple surplus-cash dividends, and a genuinely asymmetric option on the mainstream through Amazon and Henry Cavill. None of that is the issue. The issue is that at ~£204, near its all-time high and ~33× in a year of flat profit, the market already knows all of it: a blended value lands on the price, and even the analysts' targets sit right where it trades. There is no margin of safety, and the one thing that could transform the story, a hit 40K series, is precisely what no model can yet capture. So it is a watch, not a buy here: hold and admire if you own it, and treat any wobble, tariffs, a soft release window, a momentum unwind, toward ~£175 as the chance to own the quality and the option together at a discount. With a business this good, patience on price is the whole game.
