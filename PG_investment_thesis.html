01

The business

P&G sells the things people buy without thinking: Tide and Ariel detergent, Pampers nappies, Gillette razors, Crest and Oral-B, Olay, Pantene, Head & Shoulders, Dawn, Bounty, Charmin, Always. Around 65 leading brands across five segments, Fabric & Home Care, Baby/Feminine/Family Care, Beauty, Health Care and Grooming, almost all of them daily-use staples bought in good times and bad. The strategy is deliberately narrow: a focused portfolio of essential categories where, in P&G's phrase, "performance drives brand choice," backed by enormous scale in advertising, distribution and R&D.

That is the moat: shelf dominance, brand habit and a cost base no private-label rival can match at scale. It does not produce fireworks. It produces a river of cash, year after year, recession or not, which P&G converts into one of the most reliable dividend streams in the history of public markets. This is a holding you own for durability, not dazzle.

02

The growth problem, stated plainly

The honest weakness is the top line. Organic sales growth has slowed to a low-single-digit crawl and turned choppy: +2 percent, then 0 percent, then +3 percent across the last three quarters, with global market share slipping about 30 basis points as competitors lean on promotion and shoppers trade down. Much of the growth has been price rather than volume, and tariffs and a strong dollar are live headwinds for a group that earns roughly half its sales abroad.

Organic sales growth, by quarter
Organic sales growth: FY25 Q3 +1%, Q4 +2%, FY26 Q1 +2%, Q2 0%, Q3 +3%.

None of this threatens the business, P&G has now strung together 40 consecutive quarters of organic growth and is on track for a tenth straight year of core EPS growth, but it does cap the upside. A company growing organic sales ~2 to 3 percent and core earnings ~3 to 4 percent is a compounder of cash, not of the share price, unless the multiple does the work.

03

136 years of paying a dividend and 70 of raising it is not a marketing line; it is a statement about how predictable this cash flow is. The dividend is as safe as anything in the equity market.

04

Management, restructuring and the handover

Two changes are worth weighing. First, leadership: Jon Moeller handed the chief-executive role to Shailesh Jejurikar in January 2026, remaining chairman, a transition arriving at an awkward moment of soft growth and share pressure. Second, the "portfolio and productivity plan" announced in mid-2025: up to 7,000 non-manufacturing roles cut (about 15 percent of that workforce) by the end of fiscal 2027, a $1.5–2bn programme. Management is explicit that the savings are being reinvested into growth, brand superiority, innovation, not dropped to the bottom line. That is the right instinct for a business that needs to defend share, but it means the restructuring is a bet on reaccelerating growth rather than a guaranteed margin uplift, and execution under a new CEO carries real risk.

05

Valuation

For once in this library, valuation is the supportive part of the story. At ~$150 P&G trades around 22 times earnings, the lower end of its typical 22–25× band, in the bottom third of its 52-week range of $138–167. The ~2.9 percent yield is above its own multi-year average, a useful signal that the stock is cheap relative to its own history. The Street is constructive, an average target around $160–165 (range $142–186) with no sell ratings, implying high-single-digit upside. This is not a stretched defensive trading at a fear premium; it is a quality staple that has drifted to a reasonable price while growth worries dominate the headlines.

07

The case, both ways

Bull, at its strongest. The definition of a sleep-at-night holding: 70 years of rising dividends, ~$15bn a year returned, free cash flow productivity near 100 percent, a 0.40 beta, and genuinely recession-proof categories, all available at ~22× near the low of its range with a yield above its average and a restructuring that could quietly lift margins. In a portfolio of cyclicals and richly-priced tech, this is the ballast.

Bear, at its strongest. You are paying a quality premium for a business growing organic sales ~2 percent, losing a little share, leaning on price over volume, and facing tariffs, a strong dollar and relentless private-label competition, just as a new CEO runs a 7,000-job restructuring. Strip out buybacks and the per-share growth is pedestrian. The dividend is safe, but the total return may struggle to beat a mid-single-digit grind.

Variant perception

The market is fretting about the 0 percent organic quarter and the 30-basis-point share slip, treating P&G as a tiring ex-growth staple. But growth was never the proposition; durability of the cash return is. The variant point is that the soft top line has pushed a fortress compounder to ~22× and a ~2.9 percent yield, both at the cheap end of its own history, handing patient income investors a rare slight discount with the restructuring as a free option on margins. The risk the bears have right, though, is the ceiling, not the floor: the dividend is unmistakably safe, but with structural growth stuck at ~2–3 percent, your return is capped near yield-plus-low-single-digits unless the multiple re-rates. So the honest read is narrow and useful: a superb defensive holding at a fair-to-slightly-cheap price, bought for ballast and reliable rising income, not as a wealth compounder. Right tool, right job, modest expectations.

08

Currency, for a sterling holder

Two layers of dollar exposure here. First, the obvious one: PG is quoted and pays its dividend in US dollars, so your sterling return rides on GBP/USD over your holding period. Second, and less obvious, P&G itself earns roughly half its sales outside the US, so a strong dollar is a genuine headwind to its reported results, the FX drag management keeps citing. For a sterling holder the two can partly offset or compound depending on the move, but the practical point stands: this is an unhedged dollar income stream, to be sized alongside the portfolio's other dollar names. What would change the call

  • Upgrade to Buy on a dip below ~$148 (yield pushing toward 3%, a clearer discount to value), or on evidence that volume and share are turning and the restructuring is lifting margin, which would justify paying up for reaccelerating growth.
  • Downgrade towards Avoid if organic growth stalls near zero and share losses accelerate, tariffs or FX squeeze margins materially, or the multiple re-rates up toward 25×+ without a growth pickup.
11

Verdict

A fortress income holding at a fair-to-slightly-cheap price, bought for ballast, not for growth. Intrinsic value ~$159 against a ~$150 price. Action: Watch, a constructive defensive hold, accumulate on weakness toward ~$148.

P&G is exactly what it claims to be: one of the most dependable cash and dividend machines in the market, 70 years of rising payouts, ~$15bn a year returned, recession-proof categories, a 0.40 beta. And unusually for this library, it is not expensive, ~22× near the bottom of its range, with a ~2.9 percent yield above its own average, leaving a modest ~6 percent margin to a blended value plus a covered, growing income on top. What keeps it a watch rather than a clear buy is the ceiling, not the floor: organic growth is stuck at ~2–3 percent with share slipping, so the realistic total return is high-single-digit ballast, not compounding. Treated as what it is, the portfolio's shock absorber and reliable income, it is a high-quality holding at a sensible entry: a constructive watch, and a confident add on any drift toward ~$148. The dividend will keep rising long after the current growth wobble is forgotten.
