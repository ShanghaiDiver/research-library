01

The business

Microsoft is three businesses wearing one logo. Productivity and Business Processes is the cash machine: Microsoft 365, Office, Teams, Dynamics and LinkedIn, software that enterprises cannot easily leave. Intelligent Cloud is the growth engine: Azure plus the server estate, and the centre of the AI story. More Personal Computing is the tail: Windows, Xbox and gaming, search advertising and Surface. The through-line is an enterprise install base so embedded that Microsoft sets the terms, and increasingly a cloud platform that rents out compute by the hour.

The moat is switching costs at industrial scale, identity, productivity, developer tools and now AI all bundled into one vendor relationship that a chief information officer renews almost reflexively. The defining strategic fact of 2026 is that Microsoft is spending unprecedented sums to extend that moat into AI, and the market has decided, for now, to fear the bill rather than admire the ambition.

02

Operating drivers

Unlike Apple, Microsoft hands you the operating gauges. The one that moves the stock is Azure, growing around 39 to 40 percent, with an AI run-rate that passed $37bn and is up 123 percent year on year. Microsoft Cloud as a whole runs past $50bn a quarter, up in the mid-20s. Microsoft 365 Copilot, the test of whether AI actually monetises, reached roughly 15 million paid seats, up 160 percent. And the number the bulls lean on hardest: commercial remaining performance obligations, the contracted backlog, sits near $627bn, almost doubled in a year. That backlog is the evidence that the demand behind the spending is signed, not hoped for.

Read the capex and free cash flow rows together. Capital spending has gone from $21bn to $97bn on a trailing basis, nearly fivefold in five years, and free cash flow has been flat for three years despite revenue and net income soaring. Operating cash flow is booming, up roughly 30 percent to $170bn, but capex is eating almost two-thirds of every dollar of it. Free cash flow margin has fallen from 33 percent to 23 percent, and owner earnings, after the $12.4bn of share comp, sit near $60bn. ROIC has slid from 31 percent to 21 percent, because the capital going in has not yet earned its historic return. The balance sheet absorbs all this comfortably: net cash, minimal debt that is actually shrinking, one of the few AAA credit ratings left. This is a company spending its own money, not borrowed money, on a bet.

So the forward picture is high-teens revenue growth and EPS growth of 15 to 23 percent, from a 47 percent operating margin. The risk in the numbers is that depreciation, already up from $12bn to $44bn on a trailing basis, keeps climbing and turns operating leverage negative if AI revenue does not keep pace. That is the bear case in accounting form.

05

Management and capital allocation

Management is a source of stability, not risk. Satya Nadella remains chairman and chief executive with Amy Hood as a long-serving and well-regarded chief financial officer, and there is no succession overhang of the kind Apple now carries. The story here is the deliberate pivot in how the cash is spent. A decade of steady buybacks has given way to a capex supercycle: net repurchases have shrunk to a token level, barely offsetting dilution, while the dividend grows around 10 percent a year for a roughly 1 percent yield on a low-20s payout. The $69bn Activision acquisition in FY24 and now $190bn of annual AI infrastructure spending mark a management team choosing to plough cash into the future rather than hand it back. That is the right instinct if the returns are there, and a costly mistake if they are not, which is precisely the judgement an owner has to make.

06

Valuation, in context

Against its own history Microsoft is cheap. The trailing P/E of 22.6 is the low of its five-year range, which ran from roughly 26 to 38, after a de-rating that took the stock down about 18 percent this year from an all-time high near $555. EV/EBITDA at 15.6 and price to operating cash flow at 16.6 are likewise near the bottom of their ranges. The one expensive-looking metric is price to free cash flow at 39 times, and that is the whole argument: it is high only because capex has temporarily crushed the denominator.

Microsoft also has the best demand visibility of any of them, the $627bn backlog. You are paying the lowest multiple in the room for arguably the highest-quality position in it.

Intrinsic value: a discounted cash flow

Microsoft is harder to value than Apple precisely because free cash flow is distorted, and the honest way to show that is three lenses that disagree.

That spread is the point. On today's depressed free cash flow the stock looks dear; on normalised cash generation it looks roughly fair; on earnings it looks cheap. Where you land depends entirely on whether you believe the capex earns its return. At $379 the market is pricing somewhere between the normalised-FCF and earnings lenses, so it is not pricing the bear case of suppressed cash flow forever.

On trusting this

Every lens here turns on the capex question, so the DCF is even more assumption-driven than usual. The reported $97bn cash capex and the ~$190bn "total" figure differ because the larger number includes leased capacity; free cash flow uses the cash figure. Treat the scenario weights as a starting point to argue with, not a precise answer.

08

The case, both ways

Bull, at its strongest. A 47 percent operating margin, mid-to-high teens growth, Azure compounding near 40 percent, a $627bn contracted backlog that few rivals can match, and an AAA balance sheet funding the whole buildout from internal cash rather than debt. The de-rating to 22 times has handed you a quality compounder at the low end of its own valuation and below most of its peers.

Bear, at its strongest. $190bn of annual capex squeezing free cash flow at the exact moment the market has turned on AI spending. A depreciation wave that will press on EPS regardless. ROIC already sliding from 31 to 21 percent. The OpenAI exclusive agreement, once the centrepiece of the Azure AI thesis, has been terminated and restructured, with reports of OpenAI missing targets; capacity bottlenecks forced Microsoft to seek capacity from Oracle, where talks collapsed, and then Amazon; and Anthropic is reportedly building products Microsoft expected to own. If AI demand disappoints, the spend becomes a value-destroying anchor.

Variant perception

The entire stock is a referendum on one number, $190bn of capex, and the market has de-rated it to 22 times out of fear the money will not earn its keep, the same fear that hit Oracle and the chipmakers. The variant question is whether Microsoft's spend is different from the speculative AI plays. The unusual evidence is concrete: a $627bn contracted backlog, an AI run-rate up 123 percent, funded from $100bn-plus of internal net income and a AAA rating rather than borrowed money. If you judge that backlog to be real, contracted demand rather than circular, vendor-financed revenue, you are being paid to wait at 22 times. If you judge it the same bubble in a better balance sheet, the de-rating has further to run. Decide which before you buy the dip.

What would change the call

  • Downgrade towards Watch/Reduce if Azure growth decelerates below ~30%; if capex keeps rising without revenue or free-cash-flow conversion (negative operating leverage); if the partner relationships deteriorate materially; or if free cash flow fails to begin recovering by FY27–28.

12

Verdict

Quality: top-tier. Price: de-rated to ~22×, the low of its range. Intrinsic value: ~$300–650 depending on the capex payoff, weighted ~$470. Action: Buy, accumulate on weakness, eyes open on the bet.

This is the mirror image of Apple. There you overpay for slowing growth and outsourced AI; here you underpay, against the company's own history and a conservative blend, for high-teens growth, a 47 percent margin, a fortress AAA balance sheet and the best demand visibility in the group. The market has handed you 22 times earnings because it is frightened of $190bn of capex, and that fear is the opportunity if the $627bn backlog is real. Be honest that it is a genuine bet, not a free lunch: if AI demand disappoints, the spend becomes an anchor and the depreciation wave hits earnings regardless. So size it as a conviction position rather than a riskless one, and accumulate into the capex-scare weakness rather than chasing strength. A Buy, with the bet named out loud.
