01

The business

BAE Systems is one of the West's essential defence contractors, and almost uniquely diversified across domain and geography. It builds combat aircraft (Typhoon, and about 15 percent of every F-35), nuclear and conventional submarines (Astute, Dreadnought, and the AUKUS boats), warships (Type 26 frigates), armoured vehicles, munitions, naval guns, and a deep electronic-systems business in the United States, plus cyber and intelligence. Crucially, roughly 45 percent of sales are American, through a large US subsidiary that wins Pentagon programmes as a domestic supplier.

The moat is the nature of the work: multi-decade programmes, sovereign customers who cannot easily switch supplier, security-cleared facilities, and a backlog measured in years. This is not a fast-twitch business; it is a long-cycle compounder whose revenue is visible years out and whose customers are governments with rising budgets. The question, as with every quality name here, is what you pay.

02

The rearmament tailwind and the backlog

The structural driver is the most powerful in the library: the West is rearming. NATO members are lifting defence spending toward and beyond 3 percent of GDP, Europe is rebuilding after Ukraine, AUKUS commits the UK, US and Australia to submarines into the 2040s, and the US budget keeps growing. BAE sits in the middle of all of it. The evidence is in the order book: a record £83.6bn backlog, about three times annual sales, with £36.8bn of new orders in 2025 (a book-to-bill of 1.2) and management pointing to a pipeline of opportunity worth roughly £260bn, nine times sales.

04

Management and capital allocation

Charles Woodburn's team has built a reputation for steady execution and disciplined capital allocation: invest in capacity (a Cumbrian munitions site, a Jacksonville shiplift, microelectronics), bolt-on where it strengthens the US franchise (the Ball Aerospace acquisition added space capability), pay a progressive dividend, and run a £1.5bn buyback. Self-funded R&D is up 70 percent since 2020. The one watch-item the analysts flag is margin in Maritime, where complex shipbuilding programmes can be lumpy, but the group-level trajectory, margin up, cash strong, leverage low, is exactly what a long-cycle compounder should look like.

05

Valuation

Here is what makes BAE more interesting than Rolls-Royce or Games Workshop today: the price has come back to meet you. After tripling-plus through 2022–25, the shares have spent the last year consolidating, slipping about 16 percent from a 2026 peak near £23 to ~£19.30, flat over twelve months. At roughly 24 times forward earnings the multiple has clearly re-rated from its old low-teens, but it is no longer stretched against the growth, and analyst targets, clustered around £22-24 with a Buy consensus, sit some 15-20 percent above the price. The dividend yield, ~1.9 percent, is modest after the re-rating, so the cash return leans on the buyback as much as the payout. This is a quality compounder that has paused, not one priced for perfection at its high.

07

The case, both ways

Bull, at its strongest. A diversified, US-heavy defence prime with a record £83.6bn backlog (three years of sales), durable ~10 percent growth with margin expansion, a fortress balance sheet, a progressive dividend plus buyback, and a genuinely multi-decade tailwind, all available ~16 percent below its recent high with analysts pointing ~15-20 percent higher. Growth here is contracted, not hoped for.

Bear, at its strongest. The multiple has re-rated to ~24×, near the top of BAE's history, on a ~1.9 percent yield, and the 2026 outlook drew a wave of analyst downgrades to Hold over "slightly soft" guidance and Maritime margins. Defence spending is ultimately political: a Ukraine settlement or budget fatigue could trigger a sector-wide "peace dividend" de-rating, and the easy re-rating gains are already banked.

Variant perception

Both sides agree the backlog and the tailwind are real; the debate is whether ~24× is a peak multiple or a fair one for a multi-decade cycle. The variant point is that the market has spent a year quietly fretting that the re-rating ran ahead of itself, hence the flat shares and the Hold downgrades, and in doing so has handed a ~16 percent pullback in a business with more forward visibility than almost anything in this library: three years of backlog and a contracted £6bn-plus three-year cash target. The real question is not whether BAE grows, the order book answers that, but whether Western defence spending sustains long enough to hold the multiple, or whether a single ceasefire headline triggers a de-rating. The honest read: the structural case (AUKUS into the 2040s, US programmes, European rearmament) underpins the backlog regardless of any one ceasefire, so the bear's "peace dividend" would likely hit the multiple harder than the earnings. You are paying a full but not extreme price for genuine, contracted, multi-decade growth, with the margin of safety coming from the pullback and the cash returns rather than from a cheap starting multiple. A constructive entry into a structural winner, the closest a defence name comes to a buy here.

08

Currency, for a sterling holder

A sterling quote over substantially dollar-earned profit. BAE is priced in pence and pays its dividend in pounds, so no conversion friction for you, but the US dollar is its most significant currency, the company spells out that a 5-cent move in GBP/USD shifts sales by ~£500m, EBIT by ~£70m and EPS by ~1.4p. With roughly 45 percent of sales American, a weaker pound flatters reported results and a stronger pound trims them. So the holding is clean at the level of your quote and income, but the earnings underneath carry real, only-partly-hedged dollar sensitivity, as with Rolls-Royce and Games Workshop.

What would change the call

  • Upgrade to Buy on any further weakness toward or below ~1,850p (a clearer discount to value with the backlog intact), or on continued order momentum and margin progress that confirm the multiple is sustainable.
  • Downgrade towards Avoid if a credible peace settlement or budget retrenchment threatens the spending cycle, Maritime margins disappoint materially, or the multiple re-rates back toward 27×+ without faster growth.
11

Verdict

A structural winner that has paused for breath, the most comfortable entry on a quality name in this book. Intrinsic value ~1,990p against a ~1,930p price. Action: Watch, close to a Buy, accumulate on weakness below ~1,900p.

BAE has almost everything you want in a long-cycle compounder: a record £83.6bn backlog that turns geopolitics into contracted revenue, durable ~10 percent growth with expanding margins, a fortress balance sheet upgraded to A3, a progressive dividend plus buyback, and the most powerful structural tailwind in the library. And, unusually, the price has come back to meet it, ~16 percent off its 2026 high and flat over the year, leaving the shares fair-to-modestly-cheap on a blended view and ~15-20 percent below where analysts see them. It is not a deep bargain, the multiple has re-rated and the yield is slim, and the cycle carries genuine political risk, which is why it is a constructive watch rather than an outright buy. But of every quality name here, this is the one whose pullback has done the most to restore a sensible entry. Accumulate on any further weakness toward ~1,900p; the backlog means you are buying contracted growth, not a hope.
