01

The business

Amazon is a high-margin cloud and advertising business strapped to the world's largest low-margin retailer, and the strap is the point. The retail machine, online stores and third-party marketplace, generates enormous revenue at thin margins. AWS, the cloud unit, is the profit engine. Advertising, tucked inside the retail segments, is a fast-growing, very high-margin business that has quietly become one of the largest ad platforms on earth. Underneath sits Prime, the subscription flywheel that ties it together.

The moat is scale and integration: fulfilment density few can match, a cloud platform with its own custom silicon, and an advertising business riding proprietary purchase-intent data. The defining fact of 2026 is the same one stalking every hyperscaler, an unprecedented capital-spending bet on AI, except Amazon has taken it furthest, to a record $200bn for the year, and is the only one of the group generating no free cash flow as a result.

02

Operating drivers

The single most important fact about Amazon's economics is hidden in the segment split: AWS is 18 percent of revenue but well over half of operating profit. The retail businesses turn $588bn of sales into about $34bn of profit; AWS turns $129bn into $46bn, at a roughly 35 percent margin. Advertising, around $65bn a year and growing 22 percent, sits inside the retail segments at high margin and is a third profit engine in its own right. So you are really buying three businesses: a cloud jewel, an ad jewel, and a giant retailer that mostly funds itself.

The worry inside the good news is that Amazon, the cloud pioneer, is now growing its cloud slower than both major rivals.

03

Financial quality

The operating turnaround is real and impressive: operating margin has gone from 2.4 percent in 2022 to 11.5 percent, the best in Amazon's history, on cost discipline, the AWS and advertising mix, and logistics efficiency. The trouble is two lines: free cash flow and the quality of the headline profit.

Flagged: earnings quality

Reported net income is flattered. A large slice of it, roughly $15bn in FY25 and $28bn on a trailing basis, is non-operating accounting gains on Amazon's stake in Anthropic, which are volatile and non-cash. Strip them out and operating earnings are lower, putting the "clean" P/E closer to the high-30s than the headline ~27×. Combined with negative free cash flow and ROIC of only ~11 percent, the headline multiple understates how richly the operating business is priced.

05

Management and capital allocation

Andy Jassy, who built AWS, has run Amazon since 2021, with Jeff Bezos as executive chair, and the culture remains the one Bezos set: invest relentlessly ahead of the harvest, accept thin or negative cash flow for years to widen the moat. That instinct built AWS, Prime and advertising, so it has earned real credibility. The 2026 test is whether $200bn, more than any company has ever committed in a single year, is that same playbook at a new scale or a step too far. There is no dividend and effectively no buyback; every spare dollar, and some borrowed ones, goes back into the machine. For an owner, the entire thesis reduces to whether you trust this team to eventually convert the spending into cash, having watched them defer that moment repeatedly before.

06

Valuation, in context

The multiple has compressed for years, from absurd levels to roughly 27 times forward earnings, and the stock has fallen about 10 percent this year and roughly 10 percent below its November high near $258. But "cheaper than it was" is not the same as cheap. Free cash flow is negative, so the cash-based multiples do not compute, and the earnings multiple flatters because of the Anthropic gains.

08

The case, both ways

Bull, at its strongest. A genuine margin transformation to record levels, an AWS franchise with a real custom-silicon cost edge that is reaccelerating, an advertising business compounding above 20 percent at high margin, and a management team whose invest-ahead instinct built three giants. Value AWS and advertising alone and you arguably get the retailer and a $200bn AI option for little. The de-rating has handed you the highest-quality asset base in retail and one of the best in cloud.

Bear, at its strongest. No free cash flow, and consensus says none in 2026 either, with a record $200bn capex partly funded by new debt. ROIC of only 11 percent. AWS growing slower than both major cloud rivals. Reported earnings flattered by Anthropic gains. And a decade-long pattern in which the cash-flow harvest is always one more investment cycle away. If AI demand or returns disappoint, an unprecedented amount of capital is at risk.

Variant perception

The market lumps Amazon in with the AI-capex names and de-rates it on the $200bn spend. The variant question is whether you are buying a capex bet or a sum-of-the-parts mispricing: AWS at a 35 percent margin plus an ad business growing 22 percent plausibly justify most of the $2.5tn, leaving the retailer and the AI option nearly free. The bear's retort is that this framing has been true for years and the free cash flow never arrives, because Amazon always finds the next thing to build. So the real question is temperament: do you trust Jassy to harvest, or is "always investing, never harvesting" the permanent condition. At negative free cash flow and a full earnings multiple, you are paying to find out.

11

What would change the call

  • Upgrade to Buy if AWS sustains 24 percent-plus growth, capex visibly peaks, and free cash flow turns clearly positive, the combination that would prove the spending is converting.
  • Downgrade towards Avoid if AWS decelerates below 20 percent, capex keeps climbing without a margin or revenue payoff, free cash flow stays deeply negative, or the Anthropic-stripped earnings disappoint.
12

Verdict

Quality of the jewels: exceptional. Cash generation: none, by design. Intrinsic value: ~$195–350 on the capex outcome, weighted ~$270. Action: Watch, with a clear buy trigger.

This is neither Apple nor Microsoft. The crown-jewel assets, AWS and advertising, are genuinely world-class, and the de-rating to $233 has made the stock more interesting. But you are paying about 27 times earnings, closer to the high-30s once the Anthropic gains are stripped out, for a business that generates no free cash flow, is part debt-funding a record $200bn capex, and whose cloud unit is growing slower than both major rivals. The sum of the parts roughly supports the price and the weighted value sits modestly above it, but a 16 percent gap resting on a cash-negative capex bet is not a wide enough margin of safety to commit. The discipline is to wait for the signal, AWS sustaining its reacceleration and free cash flow turning positive, and buy then. A constructive watch, not a buy at this price.
